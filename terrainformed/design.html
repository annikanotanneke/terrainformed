Project Narrative

Project Goals

The goal of this project is to provide a public, interactive art installation that will allow people to confront the otherwise inaccessible elements of space travel that are already inherently tied to our lives. We rely on the services of these orbital launch rockets, whether it be for 5G internet or a fancy self-driving car, but rarely (if ever) have the opportunity to get up close and personal with the very vehicles that make such services available. This is a problem as Michigan very well could become center stage for the launching of these vehicles.

I want this art installation to provoke discourse about humanity’s relationship with space as it evolves from exploratory to commercial, and underscore the value of “ordinary” people’s voices in monumental decisions, such as becoming the next a “hub” for space cargo runs. It is not my intention to sway people one way or the other – but rather for them to advocate for their own agency in this new territory so the decision is not solely that of millionaires and lobbyist organizations. Regardless if people leave my installation “pro” or “anti” spaceport, I want people in the Lower Peninsula an active stance in something that can and will affect their immediate future, as their northern neighbors already doing right now.

Installation Description

Medium

This project will be an interactive art installation located in a public park in the Lansing area. The installation will be comprised life-size rocket “debris” made out of scrap metal. The debris will be structured in a way that people can freely interact with it how they choose – including touching, walking/climbing on (within reason), or simply observing. On the exterior/interior of some of the debris (depending on type), there will be phrases made up of text from the rocket name and graffiti, which are intended to prompt discussion specifically about the commercial space race. Next to each piece of rocket debris will be a sign that contains information about Granot Loma, MAMA, the impact of spaceports (both their benefits and potential drawbacks), and the Michigan Launch Initiative.

Potential Location(s)

I intend to install this art display in a green area of public park, in an area that will provide enough space for audience interaction.

LANSING

Since Lansing is where Michigan's state government is located, I want to have this project to be installed in one of its city parks. At this time, I have identified Durant Park and Reutter Park as areas of interest for installation. Further information about the scope of this project is required before I approach the Parks and Recreation Department. Furthermore, if further research reveals that the spaces in these parks are inadequate, I may have to look elsewhere in Lansing.

DETROIT / GRAND RAPIDS / MUSUEM

Depending on the success of this project, I would like to be able to reuse the installation and have it travel to major cities/locations like Detroit, Grand Rapids, et cetera, or create new space debris structures to stage in these cities. This, however, would require additional planning that would happen after the installation and use of the Lansing space debris project.

Intended Audience Experience

STEP ONE: Noticing

A family goes to the park to enjoy the sunny weather. The adults chat on a bench, the kids go wild on the playground, et cetera. It is, at first, a typical day at the park. But something is different about the park today. Just beyond the swingset, someone notices a hunk of metal embedded in the grass. It is so large, they can't believe that they didn't notice it when they first arrived at the park. Calling the rest of their family over, they go to investigate.

STEP TWO: Investigating

After approaching the object, it's still not totally clear what the family is looking at. Everyone is full of questions: Is that a real rocket? What's it doing in Lansing? Why haven't I heard about this? Fortunately, there is a sign placed in front of it, explaining that it is part of a public art installation, and that they are free to interact with it as they choose.

STEP THREE: Interacting and Learning

Now knowing that the rocket is supposed to be there, the family begins to interact with the installation. The kids, overseen by a parent, clamber around the fake debris and explore, while the other parent reads the sign to learn more about the art project and why such a thing would be installed in a park in the first place… The installation is a source of fun, curiosity, and conversation for the rest of the day.

STEP FOUR: Pondering

After spending their time at the park interacting with the art installation, the family goes home, but the impact of the installation has not left them. They are left wondering what it would mean for actual rockets to be launched from Michigan, even if they are far north in the Upper Peninsula. Each family member ponders the meaning of the rocket.

STEP FIVE: Action

In the days and weeks after visiting the art installation, the family tells other people they know about it. The parents their friends and coworkers, the children their friends at school. Discussion of the rocket and Granot Loma, ranging from shallow to deep, grows exponentially.

Ultimately, the goal of this installation is to provoke conversation and action. I consider any conversation about it to be a success, largely because nobody in my life is talking about it. I hope that this installation will facilitate conversation not only about Granot Loma and the issues surrounding it, but also about the dawn of the Commercial Space Age and what boundaries we should set for it.
