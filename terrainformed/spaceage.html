1 the commercial space age

Many of us are familiar with the famous “space race” between the US and USSR that gripped the world in the mid-20th century. Now that the world powers have exhausted their megalomania in the scientific realm, the competition to put giant hunks of metal into orbit has been passed over to the indubitably more concerning and self-serving party: the private sector.

We are now living in the commercial space age.

space-for-earth economy

The big-ticket item: satellites. Satellites, satellites, satellites. Satellites to guide your self-driving car, satellites to sell pictures of busy parking lots to hedge fund investors (Hsu, 2019), satellites to get you 5G. Launching low-earth orbit (LEO) satellites is no longer a far-fetched fantasy investment for big companies – it's a necessity to keep the edge in a “data-driven” market (Hsu, 2019).

“'Data is everything these days.’” (wharton, Menon)

Explain what this means for the project

The bottom line is that we thrive on information. Whether it's the gossip about your next-door neighbor or the what you aunt likes on Facebook, we desperately and instinctively strive for what we don't know, often at the price of our own morals. Data in-of-itself is lucrative, both monetarily and psychologically. Pure greed may not be the only cause of companies' willingness to sell out their customers and essentially, legally spy on the public using low-earth orbital satellites. Despite being a nationalistic Cold War manuever at its birth, humanity's relationship with space has always been data-driven, it's just that this time the data is about us, not the pretty star clusters that have graced our skies for billions of years. ** is this true?
